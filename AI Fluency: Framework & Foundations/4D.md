# Notes on Generative AI, AI Fluency, and Effective Prompting

## Overview of Generative AI

Generative AI is a type of artificial intelligence that can create new content. Instead of only sorting, labeling, or analyzing existing information, generative AI can produce original text, images, code, summaries, explanations, and other outputs based on a user’s request.

A simple way to understand the difference is:

```text
Traditional AI = identifies or classifies information.
Generative AI = creates new content from patterns it has learned.
```

For example, traditional AI might detect whether an email is spam. Generative AI could write a new email response for you.

## What Made Generative AI Possible

Generative AI became powerful because of three major developments:

### 1. Better Algorithms

Modern AI systems became much stronger because of advances in model architecture. One major breakthrough was the transformer model, which made it easier for AI to work with long pieces of text and understand relationships between words across a large context.

### 2. Massive Amounts of Data

AI models need huge amounts of text, code, documents, and other digital information to learn patterns. The growth of the internet, public websites, code repositories, and digital documents provided the raw material needed to train large models.

### 3. More Computing Power

Training large AI models requires very powerful computers. Improvements in hardware, especially GPUs and specialized chips, made it possible to train models on extremely large datasets.

## How Generative AI Works

Generative AI usually works through three broad stages:

### Pre-Training

During pre-training, the model studies large amounts of text and learns patterns in language. A major part of this process is learning to predict what is likely to come next in a sequence.

### Fine-Tuning

After pre-training, the model is adjusted so it can better follow instructions, respond helpfully, and avoid unsafe or harmful behavior.

### Deployment

After training, users interact with the model by giving prompts. The model then creates a response based on the prompt and the patterns it learned during training.

## Strengths of Generative AI

Generative AI can be useful because it has broad language and reasoning abilities.

Common strengths include:

* Writing and rewriting text
* Summarizing information
* Explaining concepts
* Translating between styles or levels of complexity
* Creating examples
* Helping with brainstorming
* Generating code
* Learning from examples provided in the prompt
* Connecting with tools or external data when available

## Limitations of Generative AI

Generative AI is powerful, but it is not perfect.

Important limitations include:

* It may not know about recent events after its knowledge cutoff.
* It can sometimes produce incorrect information.
* It may sound confident even when it is wrong.
* It has a limited context window.
* It can struggle with complex math or reasoning.
* It may misunderstand vague instructions.
* It needs human review for important work.

Because of these limitations, users should verify important outputs before relying on them.

# The AI Fluency Framework

AI fluency means being able to work with AI in a way that is useful, efficient, responsible, and safe.

The AI Fluency Framework is built around four major skills, often called the 4Ds:

```text
Delegation
Description
Discernment
Diligence
```

These skills work together. Being good at using AI is not only about writing prompts. It also requires knowing when to use AI, how to explain the task, how to judge the result, and how to take responsibility for the final work.

## The Three Modes of AI Interaction

There are three major ways people can work with AI:

### 1. Automation

Automation means the AI completes a specific task based on human instructions.

Example:

```text
Summarize this article in five bullet points.
```

The human gives a clear task, and the AI performs it.

### 2. Augmentation

Augmentation means the human and AI work together. The AI acts more like a thinking partner than just a task machine.

Example:

```text
Help me think through the pros and cons of using MongoDB versus Google Sheets for this project.
```

The human is still making decisions, but the AI helps analyze and organize ideas.

### 3. Agency

Agency means the human sets up the AI to handle future tasks with more independence.

Example:

```text
Every morning, check for new project updates and prepare a summary for me.
```

In this mode, the AI is not just responding once. It is configured to act on the user’s behalf under certain instructions.

# The 4Ds of AI Fluency

## 1. Delegation

Delegation means deciding what work should be done by you, what work should be done by AI, and what work should be done together.

Good delegation starts with understanding the task before giving it to AI.

Ask yourself:

```text
What am I trying to accomplish?
What parts require human judgment?
What parts are repetitive or time-consuming?
What can AI do well here?
What should I personally verify?
```

## Parts of Delegation

### Problem Awareness

Problem awareness means understanding your goal before involving AI.

Before asking AI for help, you should know what you are trying to do and why the task matters.

Poor example:

```text
Help me with this.
```

Better example:

```text
I need to turn these meeting notes into a clear weekly progress summary for my professor.
```

### Platform Awareness

Platform awareness means understanding what different AI tools can and cannot do.

Not every AI system is good at the same tasks. Some are better for writing, some are better for coding, some are better for file analysis, and some are better for research.

Good users choose the right tool for the job.

### Task Delegation

Task delegation means splitting the work between the human and the AI in a thoughtful way.

For example:

```text
AI can summarize the meeting notes.
I will check whether the summary is accurate.
AI can suggest wording.
I will decide what gets submitted.
```

Strong delegation uses both human expertise and AI capability.

# 2. Description

Description means explaining the task clearly so the AI understands what you want.

The better you describe the task, the better the output will usually be.

Description includes telling the AI:

* What you want
* Who the audience is
* What format to use
* What tone to use
* What steps to follow
* What constraints matter
* How detailed the answer should be

## Parts of Description

### Product Description

Product description means defining the final output.

This includes:

```text
Format
Length
Style
Audience
Purpose
Level of detail
Required sections
```

Example:

```text
Write a one-page study guide for beginners. Use clear headings, simple language, and short examples.
```

### Process Description

Process description means telling the AI how to approach the task.

Example:

```text
First summarize the main idea. Then list the key terms. After that, give one example for each term.
```

This helps the AI follow a better structure.

### Performance Description

Performance description means telling the AI how to behave during the interaction.

Example:

```text
Act like a patient tutor. Ask me questions before giving the answer.
```

Or:

```text
Be direct and concise. Do not over-explain unless I ask.
```

This is useful when you want the AI to act as a tutor, reviewer, coach, editor, or technical assistant.

# 3. Discernment

Discernment means judging the quality of the AI’s work.

AI outputs should not be accepted automatically. A fluent AI user checks whether the result is accurate, relevant, logical, and useful.

Ask yourself:

```text
Is this answer correct?
Does it match my request?
Is anything missing?
Is the reasoning sound?
Is the tone appropriate?
Would I feel comfortable submitting or sharing this?
```

## Parts of Discernment

### Product Discernment

Product discernment means evaluating the final output.

Check for:

* Accuracy
* Relevance
* Completeness
* Clarity
* Coherence
* Appropriateness for the audience
* Format requirements

### Process Discernment

Process discernment means looking at how the AI reached the result.

Even if the answer sounds good, the reasoning may have problems.

Check for:

* Unsupported claims
* Bad assumptions
* Logical jumps
* Missing evidence
* Misinterpretation of the task
* Ignored constraints

### Performance Discernment

Performance discernment means evaluating how the AI behaved during the interaction.

Ask:

```text
Was the AI too vague?
Was it too detailed?
Did it follow my instructions?
Did it communicate in the style I needed?
Did it ask useful clarifying questions?
```

Discernment and description work together. If the output is not good, improve the prompt and guide the AI again.

# 4. Diligence

Diligence means taking responsibility for how AI is used and what happens with AI-generated work.

Using AI does not remove responsibility from the human. If you use, submit, publish, or share AI-assisted work, you are still responsible for checking it and following the rules of your context.

## Parts of Diligence

### Creation Diligence

Creation diligence means being thoughtful about which AI tools you use and how you use them.

Ask:

```text
Is this the right AI tool for this task?
Am I giving it sensitive information?
Do I understand its limitations?
Am I using it in a responsible way?
```

### Transparency Diligence

Transparency diligence means being honest about AI’s role when disclosure is expected.

Different environments have different rules. A school assignment, workplace report, personal project, and public article may each have different expectations.

Ask:

```text
Do I need to say AI helped me?
Who needs to know?
What level of disclosure is appropriate?
```

### Deployment Diligence

Deployment diligence means verifying work before using or sharing it.

Before submitting or publishing AI-assisted work, check:

* Facts
* Sources
* Calculations
* Tone
* Formatting
* Originality
* Requirements
* Safety concerns

The human user should be able to stand behind the final output.

# Effective Prompting Techniques

Good prompting means giving AI enough guidance to produce a useful response.

A weak prompt is often too broad, vague, or missing important details.

A strong prompt gives context, expectations, structure, and constraints.

## 1. Give Clear Context

Instead of asking a broad question, explain the specific situation.

Weak prompt:

```text
Tell me about climate change.
```

Stronger prompt:

```text
Explain three ways climate change affects farming in tropical regions. Use examples from the last ten years and keep the explanation understandable for high school students.
```

Useful context can include:

* Topic
* Audience
* Location
* Timeframe
* Purpose
* Background information
* Desired depth

## 2. Provide Examples

Examples help the AI understand the pattern you want.

If you want a certain writing style, format, or level of simplicity, show examples.

Example:

```text
Here is the style I want:

Technical: “The software uses authentication protocols.”
Plain language: “The software checks that users are who they say they are.”

Now rewrite this sentence in the same style:
“The platform implements end-to-end encryption to protect data.”
```

Examples are especially useful for:

* Rewriting
* Formatting
* Tone matching
* Creating templates
* Converting technical language
* Generating consistent outputs

## 3. Define Output Requirements

Tell the AI what the final answer should include.

Instead of:

```text
Design a portfolio website.
```

Use:

```text
Create a one-page portfolio website plan with these sections: Hero, About, Skills, Projects, Experience, and Contact. Include responsive navigation, a mobile menu, a dark/light mode toggle, and a modern color palette.
```

Output requirements can include:

* Sections
* Length
* Format
* Style
* Features
* Constraints
* Audience
* Tone

## 4. Split Large Tasks Into Steps

For complex tasks, give the AI a sequence to follow.

Instead of:

```text
Analyze this sales data.
```

Use:

```text
Analyze this sales data by doing the following:
1. Identify the best-performing products.
2. Compare this quarter with the previous quarter.
3. Point out unusual changes or patterns.
4. Suggest possible explanations for the trends.
5. End with three recommended next steps.
```

Breaking the task into steps makes the answer more organized and reduces the chance that the AI skips something important.

## 5. Ask for Careful Reasoning

For important or complex problems, tell the AI to slow down and consider the situation carefully.

Example:

```text
Before giving a recommendation, carefully consider the constraints, possible risks, tradeoffs, and alternative approaches. Then give the best option and explain why.
```

This is useful for:

* Planning
* Decision-making
* Debugging
* Research
* Comparing options
* Strategy work

## 6. Give the AI a Role

You can guide the AI by assigning it a role.

Example:

```text
Explain how rainbows form as if you are an experienced science teacher speaking to a curious 10-year-old.
```

Roles help shape the tone, depth, and style of the response.

Useful roles include:

```text
Tutor
Editor
Code reviewer
Project manager
Career coach
Technical writer
Research assistant
Security analyst
Science teacher
```

## 7. Ask the AI to Help Improve the Prompt

One of the easiest ways to get better results is to ask the AI to help create the prompt.

Example:

```text
I want you to help me create a study guide from a lecture. I am not sure how to phrase the request. Can you help me write a better prompt?
```

This is useful when you know the goal but are not sure how to ask for it clearly.

# Prompting Checklist

Before submitting a prompt, check whether it includes:

```text
Goal: What do I want?
Context: What background does the AI need?
Audience: Who is this for?
Format: What should the output look like?
Tone: How should it sound?
Steps: Should the AI follow a process?
Constraints: What limits or rules matter?
Examples: Can I show what good looks like?
Verification: Do I need sources, checks, or reasoning?
```

## Strong Prompt Template

```text
I need help with [task].

Context:
[Explain the background or situation.]

Goal:
[Explain what you want the final result to accomplish.]

Audience:
[Who will read or use this?]

Format:
[Specify bullets, table, essay, report, code, checklist, etc.]

Requirements:
- [Requirement 1]
- [Requirement 2]
- [Requirement 3]

Tone:
[Professional, simple, friendly, technical, concise, etc.]

Process:
[Optional: explain the steps you want the AI to follow.]

Before finalizing, check for accuracy, clarity, and missing information.
```

## Example Strong Prompt

```text
I need help creating notes from a class handout.

Context:
The handout explains generative AI and AI fluency.

Goal:
Create study notes that I can copy into Google Docs.

Audience:
College students who are new to AI.

Format:
Use headings, short explanations, and examples.

Requirements:
- Reword the material in original language.
- Do not copy the original sentences.
- Include the main ideas.
- Keep the notes easy to study.

Tone:
Clear, simple, and academic.
```

# Final Summary

Generative AI creates new content based on patterns learned during training. It became possible because of better algorithms, massive digital data, and stronger computing power.

AI fluency means using AI effectively, efficiently, ethically, and safely.

The 4Ds of AI fluency are:

```text
Delegation = deciding what AI should and should not do
Description = explaining the task clearly
Discernment = judging the quality of AI outputs
Diligence = taking responsibility for AI-assisted work
```

Good prompting improves results. Strong prompts provide context, examples, output requirements, step-by-step instructions, careful reasoning guidance, and role expectations.

AI can be very useful, but the human user remains responsible for reviewing, verifying, and deciding how the final output is used.
